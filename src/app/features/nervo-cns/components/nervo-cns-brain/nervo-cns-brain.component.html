The Spinal Cord

The spinal cord is a long, thin, tubular bundle of nervous tissue and support cells that extends from the medulla oblongata of the brain to the level of the lumbar region. The spinal cord, protected by your bony spinal column, begins at the occipital bone and extends down to the space between the first and second lumbar vertebrae. Your spinal cord is protected by the spinal column which is called the vertebrae. Although the spinal column is somewhat flexible, some of the vertebrae in the lower parts of the spinal column become fused. The spinal cord has a varying width, ranging from 0.5 inch thick in the cervical and lumbar regions to 0.25 inch thick in the thoracic area. The length of the spinal cord is approximately 45 cm (18 in) in men and about 43 cm (17 in) long in women. It consists of nerves that carry incoming and outgoing messages between the brain and the rest of the body. It starts at the medulla oblongata and stretches to the lumbar region of the spine. It consists of the cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral and coccygeal regions. Each of these parts have spinal nerves, the cervical has 8 pairs of spinal nerves and the thoracic has 12 pairs of spinal nerves. The lumber and sacral both have 5 pairs. Last but not least, the coccygeal has only one pair. That leads us to a whopping total of 31 pairs or 62 total spinal nerves. Your spinal cord also controls your reflexes. A reflex is a quick action that your body does without telling the brain. Did you ever touch a hot stove and quickly pull your hand away, even before you could think, 'Ouch, that's hot!' That's a reflex. The sensation of heat that was picked up by your finger traveled to your spinal cord. Instead of wasting the time it would take to carry that message to your brain, your spinal cord sent a message to your arm muscles to pull away.
